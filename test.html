Welcome to Visualizing SEP

Explore the entire Stanford Encylopedia of Philosphy visually and beautifully.

This application uses network graphs to show the inter-relationships among all articles in the Encylopedia. It also provide previews of each article, with links into the full article at the SEP. For the first time, one can easily and powerfully navigate the entirety of this incredible resource.

To begin: search for an article, select a domain, or just hit "Randomize" to begin your journey through the Encylopedia with a surprise entry.

*Visualizing SEP is optimized for Chrome on the desktop. Performance on other browers and mobile devices is not guaranteed.

Article Graph Instructions

The Article Graph selects a single SEP article, and then shows every other article in the Encyclopedia that the selection is linked to in some way. The selected article is always at the center of the graph, and the linked articles spread radially around it.

Navigate the Graph

  • There are two fundamental ways to navigate the graph: Hover Mode and Click Mode. These are exclusive modes, and the graph can only be in one mode at a time.
  • The current mode is always listed in the “Graph Mode” box in the top right corner. Toggle between the two modes by single-clicking this box.
  • Regardless of the current Graph Mode, Double-click any node or title to make a new selection and reload the graph with the new selection.

Hover Mode (Preview Mode)

  • This is the default modal state of the graph.
  • In this mode, when you hover/mouseOver over a node or title, the entire graph will be re-drawn to preview the information from the hovered reference. The other nodes in the graph will be dimmed, the article details in the left sidebar will be temporarily replaced with preview information for the hovered node, and, if the hovered node also links to other articles in the same graph, a dashed line will be drawn from the hovered node to the shared links to indicate these relationships.
  • As soon as you stop hovering over that node, the graph will return to its original state. And when you hover over another node, the graph will be redrawn, etc.
  • At any point, you can always double-click a node or title to draw a brand new graph with this new node as the main selection.
  • Preview Mode provides a powerful way to quickly view the relationships in the Article Graph. However, it doesn’t really allow a deep exploration of the relationships between the selected article and the links.

Click Mode (Explore Mode)

  • Click Mode (Explore Mode) is for deep-diving into the relationships between the selected entry and the linked articles, as well as to make a more detailed inquiry into the linked articles themselves.
  • The power of this mode comes in when the user single-clicks a node or title. The graph will then be frozen in that state. Each time the user clicks a new node or title, the graph updates and is then re-frozen.
  • In this state, the user can more easily explore the details of the linked articles, can see how linked articles are themselves linked to other articles in the graph, or can open the full SEP article itself, etc.
  • When you hover over a node or title in Explore Mode, the data in the left sidebar will be replaced with a preview of the hovered article, but the main graph features remain the same. The graph itself is only re-drawn when you click a node.
  • At any point, you can always double-click a node or title to draw a brand new graph with this new node as the main selection.

Color (Same as Domain Graph)

Each article node and title is colored according to its primary domain designation, which is the domain chosen as the most representative expression of the article’s topic type. In many cases though, there are fluid boundaries between domain categories and many, perhaps most, articles are tagged as appearing in multiple domains. The color of the node and title will always flow from the article’s primary domain.

Left Sidebar (Same as Article Graph)

The left side bar contains details about a selected article. In its default state, the left side bar provides:

  • A preview of the first 500 characters or so in the full SEP article.
  • A link to read the full article on the SEP website.
  • A panel called "Explore the TOC" that is collapsed by default.
  • The Domain designation(s) of the selected article. Double-click to load domain graph for selected domain.
  • Details about the author(s), publication date(s), and approximate word count of the selected article.

“Explore the TOC”

  • This panel contains the complete Table of Contents from the full SEP article. Each heading is an active link. Single-click on a heading to link into that particular section of the article on the SEP website.
  • By default, the “Explore the TOC” panel is collapsed.
  • Single-click on the panel heading, or toggle button, to expand its contents. When you expand the “Explore the TOC” panel, the Domain panel and the Article Details panel will collapse. Single-click the “Explore the TOC” heading again to re-collapse this panel, and restore the Domain and Article Details panels.

Right Sidebar

The right side bar contains details about the linked articles. In its default state, the right sidebar provides:

  • A Count of Linked Articles. This shows the number of articles that are linked to the main selection.
  • List of Articles. This is collapsed by default.
  • Link Domains: A list of the primary domain designations for each of the linked articles, as well as a count of how many articles in that domain.
  • Link Directions: A list of the three kinds of link directions: bi-directional links, incoming links, and outgoing links; and a count of how many linked articles fall into each direction category.

“List of Articles”

  • This panel contains an alphabetical list of all the linked articles in the radial graph, with each title colored by its primary domain designation. Because this information duplicates the nodes and titles in the radial graph, it is hidden by default. But some graphs are so large that having the alphabetical list available is helpful for exploration.
  • Single-click on the panel heading, or its toggle button, to expand its contents. When you expand the “List of Articles” panel, the Link Domains and Link Directions panels will collapse. Single-click the “List of Articles” heading again to re-collapse this panel, and to restore the Link Domains and Link Directions panels.

Recent Searches (Same as Domain Graph)

  • The Recent Searches drop-down menu lists the 10 most recent searches on the site, inclusive of both the Article Graph and the Domain Graph. New searches are added/removed on a first-in/first-out basis.

Domain Graph Instructions

The Domain Graph shows the structure of the aricles in a specific domain of philosphy as it's currently represented in the SEP. That is, the Domain Graph shows how articles in one domain are all linked together: articles that are central to the domain will be clustered together, and share many links between them; while articles at the periphery of a domain will be offset from the central network.

  • When the user hovers a node or title in Click Mode, a dashed line is drawn from the title to its correponding node. This allows the user to see where that article lives within the domain network. When you single-click a title, the graph is updated with that title as the selected node and the article titles that the selected article links to are drawn into the graph. In this way, the user can explore the stucture of a domain in fine-grained detail.

Right Sidebar

The right side bar lists all of the articles in the selected domain. It has three major sections:

  • Count of Domain Articles.
  • Most Connected Nodes. This panel lists the top five most connected articles in the domain, sorted in descending order by the total number of connections within this domain. As usual: each title is colored by its primary domain designation. The number preceeding the title is the number of domain connections for that particular article.
  • List of All Domain Articles. This is an alphabetic list of every single article in the domain, where each title is colored by its primary domain designation. The number preceeding the title is the number of domain connections for that particular article.

Visualizing SEP is an interactive data visualization for exploring the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, one of the Web’s foremost resources on the history and practice of philosophy. The application uses network graphs to explore the inter-relationships of all the articles in the Encyclopedia. There have been a number of wonderful visualizations of the SEP before, but there has not been a single application that combines beauty, ease, and power of exploring the SEP in the way this application does.

Domain Taxonomy

The domain taxonomy used for grouping articles is not derived from the SEP itself. The domain taxonomy is adapted from the taxonomy from the Indiana University Philosophy Ontology Project (InPhO). Visualizing SEP uses the top-level taxonomic categories from InPhO, and mostly ignores the deeper-level sub-domain groupings that InPhO provides. The exception here is that we do use certain specific sub-domains to designate the philosophies of particular sciences (Biology, Physics, etc.). There are currently 25 individual domains.

The tagging and modeling of the articles into domains were completed with a combination of topic modeling, text search, and old-fashioned individual tagging of documents. The domain groupings should be seen more like language games with loosely fit boundaries between related articles instead of rigid determinations with bright-line differences between them.

Each article is tagged with a primary domain designation, which is the individual domain category that the article is best grouped with, as well as a general domain_tags field, which indicates other domains that the article can reasonably be tagged. In this way, an article can appear in multiple domain groups.

Development

The data infrastructure for the application was developed using Python, Pandas, Beautiful Soup, and MongoDB. The interactive aspects of the site were built with HTML, CSS, Javascript, and D3. A forthcoming article will describe the technical development of the application in detail.
